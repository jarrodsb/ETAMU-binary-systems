## Results

- My dataset features 10,000 single-planet s-type binary systems with a short integration of 750 orbits and a long integration of 10^4 orbits, in contrast to SPOCK's dataset consisting of 100,000 three-planet single-star systems with a short integration of 10^4 orbits and a long integration of 10^9 orbits of the inner planet. This should be okay, as binary star system configurations tend to become unstable more quickly than single-star systems.
- Whearas mean motion resonances (MMR) are an important orbital characteristic for SPOCK, they are less relevant for binary systems, particularly because I only simulated a single planet for my training dataset.
- Using Python multiprocessing with 11 workers, it took roughly 4.5 hours to generate a dataset of 10,000 systems.
- I trained a Random Forest Classifier model with an apparent accuracy score of 0.9925 and a Receiver Operating Characteristic Area Under the Curve (ROC AUC) score of 0.9742.
- This is seemingly better than the rho_crit_HW99 analytic baseline, which has an apparent accuracy score of 0.7585 and a ROC AUC score of 0.3852.
- The most important feature for my model by a significant margin appears to be rho, the ratio of the planetary semimajor axis to the binary semimajor axis, $a_p / a_{bin}$ which indicates how close the planet orbits to the binary.
